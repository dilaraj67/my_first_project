# Public Investment Fund (PIF) - Comprehensive Report 2015-2025

## Executive Summary

The Public Investment Fund (PIF) is Saudi Arabia's sovereign wealth fund, created in 1971 for the purpose of investing funds on behalf of the government of Saudi Arabia [2][4]. Following a major transformation in 2015, PIF has become the engine driving Saudi Arabia's Vision 2030 economic diversification program [2][10][28]. As of year-end 2024, PIF manages approximately **$913 billion** in assets [5][14], ranking as the **world's fifth largest sovereign wealth fund** [15].

**Key Highlights:**
- Assets grew from ~$150 billion (2015) to $913 billion (2024) [3][5][14]
- Operates as the financial engine for Saudi Vision 2030 [10][28]
- 80% of assets allocated within Saudi Arabia [17][18]
- Launched five giga-projects: NEOM, Red Sea, Qiddiya, ROSHN, Diriyah [24][25]
- Deployed $171+ billion since 2021 across priority sectors [5]
- Aims to reach $2.67 trillion AUM by 2030 [28]

## Organizational Structure and Governance

### Establishment and Transformation
- **Founded:** 1971 for investing funds on behalf of the Saudi government [2][4]
- **Major Transformation:** March 2015 - Oversight moved from Ministry of Finance to Council of Economic and Development Affairs (CEDA) [2][10]
- **Current Mandate:** Serve as the engine driving Saudi Arabia's Vision 2030 economic transformation [10][28]

### Leadership Structure

**Chairman:**
- **Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman** (March 2015 - Present) [2][10][19]
  - Saudi Arabia's de facto ruler since 2015 [2][19]
  - Appointed when oversight transferred to CEDA [2][10]

**Governor (Not CEO):**
- **Yasir Bin Othman Al-Rumayyan** (2015 - Present) [19][20]
  - Leads all areas of PIF operation [19][20]
  - Also serves as Chairman of Aramco Board of Directors [19]
  - Chairman of Newcastle United F.C. [19]

### Board of Directors
- Seven other PIF board members currently serve as ministers or high-level government positions [19]
- Many were appointed after King Salman succeeded to the throne [19]
- Many are close personal confidants of Mohammed bin Salman, particularly Governor Yasir al-Rumayyan [19]

### Governance Model
The governance model cascades from the Board of Directors to the Executive Management, which oversees five committees [19]:
1. Investment Committee
2. Risk Committee
3. Audit Committee
4. Nomination and Remuneration Committee
5. Compliance Committee

### Organizational Expansion
- **2015:** Staff of approximately 50 employees [3]
- **2018:** Expanded to nearly 500 employees [3]
- **2024:** Established regional offices in Cairo, Manama, Amman, and Muscat [14]

## Portfolio Composition

### Six Investment Portfolios

PIF has developed six investment portfolios - four domestic and two international [17][18][25]:

#### Domestic Portfolios

**1. Saudi Sector Development (SSD)** [17][18]
- **21%** of total assets under management
- Allocates capital across thirteen strategic sectors targeted by government
- Has capitalized more than 60 companies

**2. Saudi Equity Holdings (SEH)** [17][18]
- **32%** of PIF's assets under management
- Holds equity stakes in publicly listed and privately owned firms
- Includes stakes in Saudi Aramco (16%), Saudi National Bank

**3. Giga-Projects** [24][25]
- Large-scale projects forming complex ecosystems
- Five major giga-projects: NEOM, Red Sea Global, Qiddiya, ROSHN, Diriyah
- Significantly transform the Saudi economy

**4. Real Estate & Infrastructure** [17][18]
- Development projects catering to demand for leisure, retail, and entertainment
- Includes ROSHN residential developments

#### International Portfolios

**5. International Diversified Pool (IDP)** [17][18]
- Provides diversified asset base
- Invests in liquid and illiquid international assets

**6. International Strategic Investment Pool (ISI)** [17][18]
- Establishes strategic partnerships with innovative companies
- Localizes technologies to Saudi Arabia

### Sector Allocation

- Aerospace & Defense

- The Santiago Principles are 24 Generally Accepted Principles and Practices established in 2008 [23][26]
- Represent globally accepted standards for SWF governance, investment, and risk management [23][26]

**PIF's Status:**
- **Not a member** of the International Forum of Sovereign Wealth Funds (IFSWF) [23]
- However, has **adopted the Santiago Principles** [23]
- Published **self-assessment report** demonstrating alignment with all principles [23]

**Compliance Areas:** [23]
- Governance practices
- Risk management framework
- Internal policies

**Annual Reports:**
- Issues **full audited financial statements** within 90 days of year-end [23]
- Prepared in accordance with **International Financial Reporting Standards (IFRS)** [23]
- Audited by international audit firm [23]
- Widely distributed and posted on PIF website [23]

**Report Contents:** [23]
- Audited financial statements

## Conclusion

The Public Investment Fund has undergone a dramatic transformation from 2015 to 2025:

**Financial Growth:**
- Increased AUM from $150 billion (2015) to $913 billion (2024) - **6x growth** [3][5][14]
- On track toward $2.67 trillion target by 2030 [28]
- Became world's 5th largest sovereign wealth fund [15]
- Achieved 7.2% average annual portfolio return since 2017 [5]

**Strategic Evolution:**
- Transformed from passive government asset holder to active global investor [3][10]
- Became financial engine for Vision 2030 economic transformation [10][28]
- Launched five transformative giga-projects [24][25]
- Established 60+ companies across 13 strategic sectors [13][17]
- Deployed $171+ billion since 2021 [5]

**Geographic & Sectoral Diversification:**
- Maintains 80% domestic, 20% international allocation [17][18]
- Diversified from oil dependency into technology, tourism, entertainment, manufacturing [17][28]
- Expanded into gaming, sports, hospitality, electric vehicles [13][14][30][34]

**Governance & Transparency:**
- Adopted Santiago Principles despite not being IFSWF member [23]
- Publishes audited annual reports per IFRS standards [23]
- Governance concerns exist regarding unilateral decision-making [19]

**Challenges:**
- Some international investments unprofitable (LIV Golf: $1.1B+ losses) [31]
- Sportswashing criticism for sports investments [30]
- Governance structure concentrates power in Crown Prince [19]

**Future Direction:**
- Targeting $2.67 trillion AUM by 2030 [28]
- Continued focus on domestic economic transformation (80/20 rule) [17][18]
- Expansion in MENA region with new offices [14]
- Technology localization and strategic partnerships [17][18]
- Completion of giga-projects critical to Vision 2030 [24][25]
